Understanding E-Waste

Electronic waste is one of the fastest-growing pollution challenges of our time. Here's what you need to know.

What is E-Waste?

E-waste (electronic waste) refers to discarded electrical or electronic devices — from smartphones and laptops to refrigerators and televisions. When these devices reach end-of-life, they become a complex mix of valuable and toxic materials.

Improper disposal of e-waste can leach dangerous chemicals into soil and groundwater, harming ecosystems and human health. Yet over 95% of India's e-waste is handled by informal workers without protective equipment.

Fast-Changing Technology

The rapid pace of technological advancement means devices become obsolete faster, drastically increasing e-waste volumes year over year.

🌱

Environmental Contamination

Toxic substances from improperly disposed electronics contaminate soil, water, and air — affecting agriculture, wildlife, and human communities.

🏥

Public Health Risks

Informal recycling workers — often without protective gear — face serious risks from toxic fumes, heavy metal exposure, and unsafe working conditions.

Sustainable Solutions

A circular economy approach to electronics can dramatically reduce e-waste. Here's how we can all contribute.

🚫
Reduce

Buy fewer electronics. Resist the urge to upgrade devices unnecessarily. Choose quality products with longer lifespans and repairability ratings.

🤝
Reuse

Donate functional devices to schools, NGOs, or community centres. Sell or give away working electronics instead of discarding them prematurely.

🔧
Refurbish

Extend product life through repair and refurbishment. Replace batteries, screens, or components instead of buying new devices. Support right-to-repair initiatives.

♻️
Recycle

Dispose of end-of-life electronics only at CPCB-authorized e-waste collection centres. Manufacturer take-back programs offer safe and legal recycling.

E-Waste Management Rules, 2016

India's landmark regulation mandating Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) for e-waste. Producers must collect and recycle a minimum percentage of e-waste generated from their products.

CPCB Authorization

The Central Pollution Control Board authorizes e-waste recyclers and dismantlers. Only CPCB-authorized facilities may legally process e-waste in India under the EPR framework.

E-Waste Amendment Rules, 2022

Strengthened targets: producers must collect 60% of e-waste in Year 1, scaling to 70% and 80% over subsequent years. Penalties for non-compliance have been significantly increased.

Basel Convention

India is a signatory to this international treaty controlling the trans-boundary movement of hazardous waste, including e-waste, to protect developing nations from becoming dumping grounds.
